Paragraph 11: Chapter 1 — Product Foundation
Paragraph 12: 1.1 Executive Summary
Paragraph 13: Aura Health is a women's health management platform that combines cycle and symptom tracking with AI-driven personalization to help users understand their bodies, manage reproductive and hormonal health conditions, and build sustainable daily routines. The MVP targets a full-stack web application (React frontend, Node/Express backend, MongoDB) with a mobile-responsive design, launching first in India (DPDP-compliant) with an architecture ready for broader regional expansion.
Paragraph 14: 1.2 Product Vision & Mission
Paragraph 15: Vision: A world where every woman understands her body well enough to advocate for her own health, supported by technology that adapts to her — not the other way around.
Paragraph 16: Mission: Deliver an accessible, medically grounded, AI-personalized health companion that tracks cycles, symptoms, and mood; surfaces actionable insight; and connects users to community and clinical support when they need it.
Paragraph 17: 1.3 Problem Statement
Paragraph 18: Existing cycle-tracking apps focus narrowly on fertility/period prediction and ignore chronic conditions such as PCOS, endometriosis, and PMDD.
Paragraph 19: Symptom and mood data is siloed from actionable guidance — users log information but rarely get personalized next steps.
Paragraph 20: Condition-specific communities are fragmented across generic forums and social media, offering low-quality, unmoderated advice.
Paragraph 21: Caregivers (partners, guardians) and clinicians (doctors) have no structured way to view relevant health context with the user's consent.
Paragraph 22: Non-English-first, lower digital-literacy users are underserved by existing apps' UX complexity.
Paragraph 23: 1.4 Market Analysis
Paragraph 24: The global femtech market was valued in the low tens of billions of USD as of the mid-2020s and is projected to grow at a double-digit CAGR through the end of the decade, driven by increased health awareness, rising smartphone penetration, and growing willingness to pay for subscription health tools. India specifically represents a large underserved segment: a young population, rising female smartphone ownership, and limited access to gynecological specialists outside metro areas create strong demand for a digital-first triage and education layer. Key trends shaping the category include a shift from pure period-tracking toward holistic hormonal-health platforms, growing regulatory scrutiny of health-data privacy, and rising interest in AI-personalized care plans.

Paragraph 39: 1.7 User Research Findings
Paragraph 40: Formative interviews and secondary research (to be validated with primary user studies pre-launch) point to three consistent findings: (1) users abandon cycle-tracking apps within weeks when logging feels like a chore with no payoff; (2) users with diagnosed conditions (PCOS, endometriosis, PMDD) want condition-specific guidance, not generic period prediction; (3) users are willing to share data with a partner or guardian only when they retain granular, revocable control over exactly what is shared.

Paragraph 151: Chapter 3 — Complete User Journeys
Paragraph 152: 3.1 Primary Flow (New Patient User)
Paragraph 153: Visitor → Landing Page → Explore Features → Register → Verify Email → Onboarding Survey (persona + condition selection) → AI Routine Setup → Dashboard → Daily Check-in → Community Access (optional) → View Reports → Settings → Logout.
Paragraph 154: Decision points: at Registration, an invalid/duplicate email routes to an inline error with a 'resend verification' path. At the Onboarding Survey, selecting a diagnosed condition (PCOS/endometriosis/PMDD) branches into a condition-specific question set before the standard baseline questions resume.
Paragraph 155: 3.2 Partner User Journey
Paragraph 156: Invited (via link/email from primary user) → Accept Invitation → Create Partner Account → Consent Scope Confirmation (view what primary user has shared) → Partner Dashboard (cycle-phase summary, 'how to support today' tips, no raw symptom logs unless explicitly shared) → Notification Preferences → Logout.
Paragraph 157: Alternative path: Partner declines invitation → primary user notified invitation was not accepted, can resend or cancel.
Paragraph 158: 3.3 Guardian User Journey
Paragraph 159: Create Guardian Account → Add Linked Minor Profile (identity/relationship verification) → Set Alert Preferences (red-flag symptoms, missed-period thresholds) → Guardian Dashboard (alert feed only, no full diary access) → Escalation flow if a red-flag alert fires (in-app notification + optional resource links for seeking care) → Logout.
Paragraph 160: 3.4 Admin User Journey
Paragraph 161: Admin Login (MFA required) → Admin Console Home → Moderation Queue (flagged community posts, reported users) → Take Action (approve/remove/warn/ban) → User Management (search, verify doctor credentials, suspend accounts) → System Analytics Dashboard → Audit Log Review → Logout.
Paragraph 162: 3.5 Returning User (Login)
Paragraph 163: Landing Page → Login → Credential Validation → (if MFA enabled) OTP Verification → Dashboard. Error state: invalid credentials show inline error after 3 attempts, a CAPTCHA challenge appears; after 5 attempts, temporary lockout with a password-reset prompt.
Paragraph 164: 3.6 Forgot Password Flow
Paragraph 165: Login Screen → 'Forgot Password' → Enter Email → Reset Link Sent Confirmation → User Opens Email → Reset Link (expires in 30 minutes) → New Password Form (strength validation) → Success → Redirect to Login. Error states: expired link shows a 'request a new link' prompt; reused link is rejected with the same prompt.
Paragraph 166: 3.7 Account Deletion Flow
Paragraph 167: Settings → Account → Delete Account → Confirmation Modal (explains data retention/deletion timeline per DPDP) → Re-authentication (password re-entry) → Final Confirmation → Soft-delete (30-day grace/recovery window) → Hard delete after grace period → Confirmation email.
Paragraph 168: 3.8 Survey Retake Flow
Paragraph 169: Settings → Health Profile → 'Retake Survey' → Warning Modal (explains this will regenerate the AI routine) → Survey (pre-filled with prior answers where applicable) → Review Changes Summary → Confirm → New Routine Generated → Dashboard Updated.

Paragraph 243: 6.1 Dashboard Loading Sequence
Paragraph 244: Step 1 — Flower Bloom: Brand mark petals rotate 360° while a plum→rose gradient fades in. Duration 1.2s, easing cubic-bezier(0.25, 0.46, 0.45, 0.94).
Paragraph 245: Step 2 — Dashboard Fade-In: Opacity 0→1 over 0.8s, 0.4s delay so it begins as the bloom animation completes.

Paragraph 253: Speaking (if voice enabled Phase 2+): audio waveform visualization synced to playback.

Paragraph 353: Chapter 9 — AI System Specification
Paragraph 354: 9.1 Architecture Overview
Paragraph 355: Personalization is delivered by a hybrid system: a deterministic rule engine handles anything with clinical or safety implications (red-flag detection, contraindication checks), while an LLM-based layer (via the Anthropic API) handles natural-language explanation, routine phrasing, and open-ended Q&A. The LLM never has final say on a safety-relevant recommendation — it explains or rephrases outputs the rule engine has already approved.
Paragraph 356: 9.2 Prompt Structure
Paragraph 357: Each AI call is assembled from three layers: a fixed base system prompt (product context, tone, safety constraints, explicit instruction to defer diagnosis to clinicians), a variable context block (the user's condition tags, recent 14-day log summary, current cycle phase), and the specific task instruction (e.g., 'generate today's routine' or 'explain this recommendation in under 60 words').
Paragraph 358: 9.3 Rule Engine
Paragraph 359: 9.4 Decision Tree (Routine Generation, simplified)
Paragraph 360: 1. Load user profile (condition tags, preferences, active medications).
Paragraph 361: 2. Determine current cycle phase from most recent cycle data.
Paragraph 362: 3. Run red-flag rules against last 7 days of logs — if triggered, short-circuit to escalation output only.
Paragraph 363: 4. Pull base routine template for phase + condition tag combination from the content library.
Paragraph 364: 5. Filter out any contraindicated items.
Paragraph 365: 6. Pass the filtered template + rationale bullets to the LLM layer to produce natural-language phrasing and a short 'why this today' explanation.
Paragraph 366: 7. Persist the generated routine and serve to the client.
Paragraph 367: 9.5 Mood Analyzer
Paragraph 368: Mood logs (1–5 scale plus optional free-text) are aggregated on a rolling basis against cycle-day position. When a user has at least two prior cycles of mood data, the system flags a statistically consistent low-mood window (commonly luteal-phase for PMDD-tagged users) and surfaces an early-warning card 2–3 days ahead of the historical dip, with coping-strategy suggestions drawn from the clinician-reviewed content library.
Paragraph 369: 9.6 Recommendation System
Paragraph 370: Recommendations are ranked by a simple weighted score combining: clinical relevance (condition tag match), recency of related logs, and predicted user engagement (based on historical completion rate of similar routine-item types). No recommendation is auto-surfaced from raw LLM generation without passing through the rule-engine relevance/safety filter first.
Paragraph 371: 9.7 Explanation Engine
Paragraph 372: Every AI-surfaced recommendation carries a one-tap 'Why am I seeing this?' affordance that renders the underlying rule/data trigger in plain language (e.g., 'Your logged pain has been higher than usual this week, and it's day 24 of your cycle — many people in the luteal phase find gentle movement and magnesium-rich meals helpful'), sourced from the same rationale bullets generated in the decision tree step above — never a black-box output.

Paragraph 387: Chapter 11 — MongoDB Schema
Paragraph 388: All collections use Mongoose schemas with strict mode enabled and a soft-delete pattern (deletedAt) rather than hard deletes, except where a user explicitly exercises a DPDP erasure request, which triggers a scheduled hard-delete job.
Paragraph 389: Collection: users

Paragraph 394: Validation: startDate required; endDate, if present, must be ≥ startDate

Paragraph 418: Chapter 13 — Security Implementation
Paragraph 419: 13.1 Core Controls
Paragraph 420: 13.2 Data Protection Compliance (India DPDP Act)
Paragraph 421: Explicit, granular consent captured at account creation and for every data-sharing relationship (see consents collection).
Paragraph 422: Purpose limitation: health data used only for the stated personalization/analytics purpose disclosed in the privacy notice.
Paragraph 423: Right to erasure: account deletion flow (Chapter 3.7) triggers scheduled hard-delete after a 30-day grace window.
Paragraph 424: Grievance officer contact published in-app and on the website footer, per statutory requirement.
Paragraph 425: Architecture designed so that expanding to GDPR-aligned markets later mainly requires policy/UX changes, not a data-model rewrite.
Paragraph 426: 13.3 Audit Logging
Paragraph 427: Security-relevant and consent-relevant events are written to the append-only auditLogs collection: login (success/failure), password change, consent grant/revoke, admin actions (suspend/ban/content removal), and data export/deletion requests. Logs are retained per the compliance policy and are never exposed to non-admin users.
